Exam technique

Knowing the content is only half the battle. Exam technique is how you turn knowledge into marks.

The papers

SL HL
Paper 1 45 min, multiple choice, 30 marks, 30% 60 min, multiple choice, 40 marks, 20%
Paper 2 1h 30m, short + extended response, 40% 2h 30m, short + extended response, 40%

Paper 1 tests breadth of knowledge across the syllabus. Paper 2 tests depth, application, and written communication.

Paper 1 — multiple choice

Strategy

  1. Read every option before choosing. The right answer is often the one that is more specific or more accurate — not the first plausible one.
  2. Eliminate wrong answers first. Cross out clearly incorrect options to narrow the field.
  3. Beware distractors. Exam writers deliberately include tempting wrong answers based on common misconceptions.
  4. Don't leave blanks. No penalty for guessing — answer every question.
  5. Time discipline. About 90 seconds per question. If stuck, mark it, move on, return later.

Common traps

  • "All of the above" / "None of the above" — usually avoid unless you are certain every option applies.
  • Absolute words ("always", "never", "must") — often wrong because real design involves trade-offs.
  • Similar-looking options — look for the distinguishing word. One option may be correct because it is more precise.
  • Over-reading — if the question seems simple, it probably is. Don't invent complications.

Command-style words in MCQ

Even multiple choice uses command terms. Read them carefully:
- "Identify" — name the correct item
- "Describe" — select the option that gives an accurate description
- "Define" — select the formal definition
- "Suggest" — select the best proposal for the scenario

Paper 2 — structured and extended response

Structure of answers

Paper 2 questions use IB command terms that dictate what kind of answer is expected. Address the command term directly.

Command term What to do Marks typical
State / List / Identify Give the item, no explanation 1
Define Give the precise meaning 1–2
Outline / Describe Give a brief account, with features 2–4
Explain Give reasons or causes 3–6
Justify Give reasons backing a decision 4–6
Compare / Contrast Give similarities / differences 4–8
Evaluate Weigh strengths and weaknesses, conclude 6–10
Discuss Consider multiple views with evidence 6–10

Answering "explain" (4 marks)

"Explain" wants reasoning, not just statement. A 4-mark explain question typically earns marks through:
- A correct statement (1 mark)
- A reason that statement is true (1 mark)
- A specific example or mechanism (1 mark)
- A connected consequence or implication (1 mark)

Answering "evaluate" (10 marks)

"Evaluate" requires a balanced judgement:
- Introduce the issue and its context
- Strengths / arguments for (2–3 specific points)
- Weaknesses / arguments against (2–3 specific points)
- Use specific examples, not generic statements
- Conclude with a reasoned judgement

Avoid "it depends" as a conclusion. Take a position and justify it.

Writing with specificity

Vague answers lose marks. Specific answers earn them.

❌ "The product is more sustainable because it is made of better materials."
✅ "The product uses recycled aluminium, which has ~95% lower embodied carbon than primary aluminium (~10 MJ/kg vs ~200 MJ/kg), significantly reducing its cradle-to-gate carbon footprint."

❌ "UCD is important because users matter."
✅ "UCD is important because designs that ignore user research typically address invented problems. Microsoft's Inclusive Design Toolkit shows that features designed for permanent disability (e.g. voice control) also benefit situational users (parents holding babies)."

Specificity comes from:
- Named products / companies / people
- Numbers, percentages, measurements
- Technical terms used correctly
- Cause-and-effect reasoning

Using case studies

Every topic in this book includes case studies. Build a mental library:

  • For ergonomics → OXO Good Grips, Herman Miller Aeron
  • For sustainability → Patagonia, Fairphone, Interface
  • For UCD → IDEO shopping trolley, Ford Third Age Suit
  • For manufacturing → LEGO brick, Apple unibody
  • For circular economy → Rolls-Royce Power by the Hour, Philips Pay-per-Lux

Drop them into answers by name. Examiners reward specificity — a student who says "Apple's unibody MacBook is milled from a single aluminium billet, achieving 100+ parts of structural rigidity in one component" scores higher than one who says "CNC machining is used in manufacturing".

Time management in Paper 2

Work backwards from the mark allocation:
- ~1.5 minutes per mark for SL (90 min / 40 marks)
- ~2 minutes per mark for HL (150 min / ~70 marks)

Don't spend 20 minutes on a 4-mark question. Plan, write, move on.

First pass: answer questions you know quickly.
Second pass: attempt harder questions.
Third pass: check, add examples, fix obvious errors.

Leave 5 minutes at the end for proofreading.

Common exam mistakes

  1. Not addressing the command term. "Explain" answers that only describe, "evaluate" answers that only list.
  2. Generic answers. Avoiding specific products, numbers, or mechanisms.
  3. Undefined terms. Using "sustainable", "efficient", "good" without defining them.
  4. Ignoring context. Answering in general when the question gave a scenario.
  5. Padding. Rephrasing the question back at the examiner, repeating points, vague filler.
  6. Running out of time on easy questions left to the end.

Before the exam

  • Review this book, especially case studies and glossary
  • Re-read the IB subject guide for official mark schemes
  • Practise past papers under timed conditions
  • Make one-sheet summaries per topic
  • Sleep. Tired students lose marks on things they know.

In the exam

  • Read each question twice before starting
  • Underline command terms and key nouns in the question
  • Plan multi-mark answers in 30 seconds before writing
  • Write legibly — examiners cannot mark what they cannot read
  • Use diagrams where helpful (quick pencil sketches score marks if clearly labelled)
  • Check the mark allocation — write enough, not too much
