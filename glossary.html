Glossary

Master glossary of terms used throughout the IB Design Technology 2027 course. Use this as a revision resource and quick reference.

A

Accessibility — the extent to which a product can be used by people with disabilities and varying abilities.

Actuator — an electronic component that converts an electrical signal into physical action (motor, solenoid, buzzer).

Additive manufacturing — building a part layer by layer (3D printing).

Aesthetic-usability effect — the tendency for attractive products to be perceived as more usable.

Agent of change — a designer who consciously uses design decisions to shape broader systems.

Anthropometrics — the branch of ergonomics concerned with measuring the human body.

Appearance model — a model finished to show the look of the final product without functioning internals.

B

Backlash — free play between mating mechanical parts.

Batch production — manufacture of identical units in groups.

Beam — a horizontal or transverse structural member resisting bending.

Behavioural design — design addressing pleasure and frustration during use.

Bending — load causing curvature in a structural member.

BOM — Bill of Materials; complete list of components with suppliers.

Buckling — sudden sideways failure of a slender compression member.

C

CAD — Computer-Aided Design.

CAM — Computer-Aided Manufacturing.

Cam — a shaped rotating element that drives a follower.

Capacitor — passive electronic component that stores electrical charge.

Ceramic — hard, brittle, insulating crystalline inorganic material.

Circular economy — economic model where products and materials remain in circulation through reuse, repair, remanufacture, and recycling.

Clearance — space provided so that the largest user fits without obstruction, or free space between moving and stationary parts.

CNC — Computer Numerical Control machining.

Column — a vertical structural member resisting compression.

Comparative analysis — evaluation by comparison against existing products.

Composite — material combining two or more constituents with distinct phases.

Compression — force pushing a member together.

Continuous production — uninterrupted production of a single product.

Cradle-to-cradle — LCA boundary from raw extraction through recycling into new products.

Cradle-to-gate — LCA boundary from raw extraction to factory gate.

Cradle-to-grave — LCA boundary from raw extraction to final disposal.

Critical reflection — honest acknowledgement of strengths, weaknesses, and learning.

D

Dark pattern — a design intentionally manipulating users against their interests.

Datasheet — manufacturer document specifying a component's parameters.

Decision matrix — tool for comparing candidate materials numerically against weighted criteria.

Design for disassembly (DfD) — designing products to be taken apart easily at end of life.

Design journal — ongoing documentation of decisions, sketches, tests, and reflection.

Design process — structured approach from design opportunity to tested solution.

Design specification — a measurable requirement a product must meet.

Destructive test — loading a structure to failure to determine true capacity.

DFM — Design for Manufacture; designing to be easily and economically produced.

Diode — electronic component allowing current flow in one direction.

Draft angle — taper on a moulded feature to allow release from the mould.

Dynamic data — anthropometric measurements taken with the body in motion.

E

Efficiency — MA divided by VR, expressed as a percentage; ratio of useful output to input energy.

Elasticity — ability to return to original shape after deformation.

Embodied carbon — total CO₂ emissions associated with producing a material.

EMC — Electromagnetic Compatibility.

Emotional design — design considering users' emotional responses as primary.

Emotional durability — the quality of a product that makes users want to keep it.

Empathize — the design process stage of understanding users through research.

Empathy map — tool categorising research findings into what users say, think, do, feel.

Equitable use — a universal design principle: useful to users with diverse abilities.

Ergonomics — the study of the relationship between people and the products and environments they use.

Evaluation — systematic assessment of whether a design meets its objectives.

Extrusion — forcing material through a die to produce a continuous profile.

F

Fatigue test — repeated cyclic loading to reveal long-term failure modes.

FDM — Fused Deposition Modelling; 3D printing using extruded plastic filament.

FEA — Finite Element Analysis; computational simulation of stress distribution.

Feedback loop — the cycle of build, test, learn, refine.

Firmware — software running on a microcontroller.

Fixed cost — investment independent of units produced.

Follower — mechanical member that moves in response to a cam's profile.

Forming — shaping material without adding or removing it.

Friction — resistive force opposing relative motion between surfaces.

Fulcrum — the pivot point of a lever.

Functional model — model built to demonstrate specific mechanisms or operations.

Functional obsolescence — physical failure engineered into a product.

G

Gear ratio — the ratio of teeth between driven and driver gears.

Gear train — two or more gears meshed to transmit motion.

Greenwashing — misleading sustainability claims unsupported by practice.

GWP — Global Warming Potential; CO₂-equivalent emissions.

H

Hardness — a material's resistance to surface indentation or scratching.

High-fidelity prototype — refined prototype close to final product.

I

IC — integrated circuit; many components on one chip.

Ideate — design process stage of generating multiple potential solutions.

Injection moulding — forcing molten thermoplastic into a closed mould.

Inclusive design — designing products to work for the widest possible range of users.

Input — component converting a physical quantity to an electrical signal.

Integration — combining a mechanism or circuit with the surrounding product systems.

Interview — structured or semi-structured one-to-one conversation with a user.

Iteration — a cycle of building, testing, learning, refining.

Iterative design — refining a design through repeated cycles.

J

JIT — Just-in-Time; components arrive exactly when needed.

L

LCA — Life-cycle Assessment; structured environmental impact analysis.

LCI — Life-cycle Inventory; data collection stage of LCA.

LCIA — Life-cycle Impact Assessment; conversion of inventory to impact categories.

Lean manufacturing — systematic reduction of waste in production processes.

Learnability — how quickly a new user can begin using a product.

LED — light-emitting diode.

Lever — a rigid bar pivoting about a fulcrum.

Life cycle — all stages from raw material extraction through disposal.

Linear economy — traditional take-make-dispose economic model.

Linkage — pivoting bars producing complex motion paths.

Load path — the route applied forces travel through a structure.

Low-fidelity prototype — rough, quick, inexpensive prototype.

M

Mass customisation — mass-production methods producing individualised products.

Mass production — continuous manufacture of identical units at high volume.

Material cascading — using materials through successive lower-value applications.

Material selection — process of choosing a material best meeting design requirements.

MA — Mechanical Advantage; ratio of output force to input force.

Mechanism — assembly of moving parts that transmits or transforms motion.

Metal — crystalline material typically strong, stiff, and conductive.

Microcontroller — programmable IC with processor, memory, and I/O pins.

Mock-up — full-scale non-functional representation of a design.

Model — representation communicating aspects of a design.

Monocoque — shell structure deriving strength from its outer surface.

Multimodal design — design engaging multiple senses simultaneously.

N

Near-net-shape — manufacturing to a shape close to the final part.

Nesting — arranging parts on stock material to minimise waste.

O

Observation — research by watching users in their natural context.

Ohm's Law — V = I × R.

One-off production — manufacture of a single unique unit.

Output — component converting an electrical signal to physical action.

P

PCB — Printed Circuit Board.

Percentile — a value below which a given percentage of the population falls.

Persona — evidence-based fictional user profile representing a segment of real users.

Physiological factor — ergonomic consideration relating to body function over time.

Planned obsolescence — designing products to have a limited useful life.

Poka-yoke — mistake-proofing design preventing incorrect assembly.

Polymer — long-chain molecular material, lightweight and easily shaped.

Primary research — data collected first-hand for a specific project.

Product semantics — the study of meaning communicated through product form.

Product-as-a-service (PaaS) — business model selling function rather than ownership.

Prototype — tangible, testable representation of a design idea.

Psychological factor — ergonomic consideration relating to perception, emotion, cognition.

Q

QA — Quality Assurance; designing processes to prevent defects.

QC — Quality Control; inspecting products to detect defects.

Qualitative evaluation — evaluation using descriptive observations and quotes.

Quantitative evaluation — evaluation using numerical measurements.

R

Rapid prototyping — fast, computer-controlled fabrication methods.

Reach — distance a user must extend to operate a control.

Reciprocating — back-and-forth linear motion.

Reflective design — design addressing meaning users attach to ownership.

Remanufacturing — rebuilding used products to as-new specification.

Resistor — passive electronic component limiting current.

Reverse logistics — infrastructure for returning products to manufacturer.

R-hierarchy — ranking of circular strategies from refuse to recover.

Right to repair — movement advocating legal access to repair parts and documentation.

S

Safety factor — multiplier applied to expected loads to ensure reliability.

Schematic — logical diagram of a circuit using standard symbols.

Secondary research — existing data collected by others for other purposes.

Sensor — component converting a physical quantity into an electrical signal.

Sensory design — design engaging multiple senses intentionally.

Shape memory alloy — metal returning to a pre-set shape when heated.

Shear — force causing layers to slide past each other.

Situational impairment — temporary limitation caused by context rather than condition.

SLA — Stereolithography; 3D printing using laser-cured liquid resin.

Smart material — a material responding to an external stimulus in a controlled way.

Specific strength — strength per unit mass (strength-to-weight ratio).

Specific stiffness — stiffness per unit mass.

Specification check — evaluation by comparing product against each specification point.

SPC — Statistical Process Control.

Static data — anthropometric measurements taken with the body at rest.

Strain — ratio of deformation to original length.

Stress — force per unit cross-sectional area.

Structure — assembly designed to carry loads.

Style obsolescence — aesthetic updates making older products feel outdated.

Subtractive manufacturing — removing material to form a part.

Survey — set of written questions distributed to many users.

Sustainability — meeting present needs without compromising future generations.

System boundary — scope of stages included in an LCA.

T

Take-back scheme — manufacturer retrieval of products at end of life.

Tensile strength — maximum stress a material can withstand while being stretched.

Tension — force pulling a member apart.

Think-aloud protocol — usability testing method where users verbalise thoughts.

Thermoplastic — polymer softening when heated, reshapeable.

Thermoset — polymer curing irreversibly.

Tolerance — permitted variation from a nominal dimension.

Tooling — moulds, dies, jigs, or fixtures required for a manufacturing process.

Topology optimisation — computational method removing low-stress material iteratively.

Torque — rotational force (N·m).

Torsion — twisting force.

Toughness — energy a material absorbs before fracturing.

Triangulation (research) — confirming findings using three or more research methods.

Triple bottom line — evaluation framework considering people, planet, profit.

Truss — triangulated frame where members carry only tension or compression.

U

UCD — User-Centred Design.

Universal design — design approach producing products usable by all without adaptation.

UTS — Ultimate Tensile Strength.

Usability — ease with which a user achieves goals with a product.

User journey map — visual timeline of user actions, thoughts, emotions during a task.

User need — requirement essential for a product to be functional, safe, fit for purpose.

User want — preference enhancing satisfaction but not essential.

V

Variable cost — cost scaling with units produced.

Visceral design — design addressing immediate gut responses to appearance.

VR — Velocity Ratio; ratio of input distance to output distance.

W

Waste hierarchy — ranking of waste-reduction strategies from best (refuse) to worst (dispose).

WCAG — Web Content Accessibility Guidelines.

Weighting — relative importance assigned to each selection criterion.

Working prototype — prototype demonstrating or testing functional behaviour.

Y

Yield point — stress at which permanent deformation begins.

Young's modulus — material property relating stress to strain in the elastic region.
