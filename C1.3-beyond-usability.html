C1.3 — Beyond usability (HL)

Key concepts

Usability is about can the user do the task? Beyond usability asks a deeper question: how does the user feel about doing the task, and about the product they are doing it with?

A usable product is not necessarily a product people love. Hospital waiting-room chairs are technically usable but nobody enjoys them. A well-designed smartphone is usable and emotionally compelling. The difference matters — emotional response drives what users buy, what they keep, and what they recommend.

Emotional design

Don Norman's three levels of emotional design (2004):

  1. Visceral — the immediate gut response to appearance. Pre-cognitive. "That looks beautiful / scary / inviting." Shapes first impressions in milliseconds.

  2. Behavioural — the response to use. "This is satisfying to hold / frustrating to open / elegant to operate." Develops over use.

  3. Reflective — the meaning attached to owning or using it. "This says something about who I am / what I value." The slowest, deepest level.

Successful products typically work at all three levels. An iPhone is visceral (sleek, jewel-like), behavioural (responsive, tactile), and reflective (status symbol, personal device).

Product semantics

Product semantics is the study of how products communicate meaning through form, colour, texture, and detail. Every design decision sends signals:

  • Sharp angles suggest precision, technology, speed
  • Curves suggest warmth, comfort, nature
  • Matte surfaces suggest professionalism; glossy surfaces suggest luxury or youth
  • Exposed screws suggest honesty and repair; hidden fastenings suggest refinement

Good designers use these signals intentionally. Mismatched semantics (a medical device styled like a toy) confuses users.

Aesthetic experience

Aesthetics is not decoration — it is essential. Users perceive aesthetically pleasing products as:

  • Easier to use (even when objectively they are not)
  • More trustworthy
  • Higher quality
  • Worth more money

This is the aesthetic-usability effect: users forgive aesthetic products small usability flaws that would annoy them in ugly products. Beauty is not optional.

Sensory design

Products are experienced through all senses:

  • Sight — form, colour, material, surface
  • Touch — texture, temperature, weight, vibration
  • Hearing — click, whirr, thud, silence
  • Smell — new-car smell, leather, wood
  • Taste — rare in products but matters in food, medicine, packaging

Designers who attend to multiple senses create richer experiences. The "click" of a premium pen, the satisfying weight of a well-made knife, the scent of freshly cut wood — all contribute.

Product personality and identity

Products have personalities. A Volvo feels safe, steady, honest. A Ferrari feels aggressive, intense, elite. An iPhone feels confident and minimalist. A Nintendo console feels playful and approachable.

Personality comes from consistent decisions across form, interaction, material, sound, and language. It must align with brand identity and user aspirations.

Meaning and culture

Products carry cultural meaning:

  • Heritage brands (Leica, Rolex, Moleskine) trade on history
  • Minimalist design signals quality in some contexts, coldness in others
  • Traditional craft signals authenticity and care
  • High-tech aesthetics signal modernity and progress

Designers choosing semantic directions shape user perception — powerful, and potentially manipulative.

Case studies

Apple retail experience — every surface, sound, scent, and gesture in Apple stores is designed. The tactile weight of a MacBook, the spacing of the products, the staff scripts — all work together to create aspiration and trust.

Dieter Rams and Braun — established a design language of calm functionalism. Minimal ornament, honest materials, subtle details. Influenced Apple, Muji, thousands of others. A masterclass in consistent product semantics.

Tesla Cybertruck — deliberately divisive aesthetics. Sharp, angular, dystopian. Signals disruption and technical confidence. Emotional response is polarised by design.

Fisher-Price children's products — behavioural and visceral design for children. Bright primary colours, rounded forms, tactile satisfaction. Aesthetics tuned for early-years developmental psychology.

Muji household goods — minimalism as philosophy. Products almost invisible in use, packaging stripped of branding. Reflective-level design: ownership signals identity (thoughtful, deliberate) without ostentation.

Glossary

  • Emotional design — design that considers users' emotional responses as primary to functional ones.
  • Visceral design — design addressing immediate gut responses to appearance.
  • Behavioural design — design addressing pleasure and frustration during use.
  • Reflective design — design addressing the meaning users attach to ownership.
  • Product semantics — the study of meaning communicated through product form.
  • Aesthetic-usability effect — the tendency for attractive products to be perceived as more usable.
  • Sensory design — design that engages multiple senses intentionally.
  • Product personality — the character that emerges from consistent design decisions.

Check your understanding

1. Describe Don Norman's three levels of emotional design with a product example.

Visceral: immediate gut response to appearance (iPhone's sleek, jewel-like form attracts instantly). Behavioural: response to use (iPhone's smooth gesture interactions feel satisfying). Reflective: meaning attached to ownership (iPhone ownership signals taste, wealth, or tech-fluency). The iPhone succeeds because it works at all three levels. Products that work at only one level (ugly but functional, or beautiful but frustrating) rarely achieve emotional connection.

2. Explain the aesthetic-usability effect and its implications for design.

Users perceive aesthetically pleasing products as more usable, even when measured usability is identical. Attractive products get more patience from users; they forgive small flaws that would be rejected in ugly products. The implication: aesthetics is not decoration — it is essential to usability. Designers who treat visual design as optional produce products users abandon at the first frustration. Beauty and function reinforce each other.

3. Define product semantics and give two examples of how form communicates meaning.

Product semantics is the study of meaning communicated through form, colour, texture, and detail. Example 1: sharp geometric forms suggest precision and technology — used on high-end cameras and cars. Example 2: soft curved forms suggest warmth and approachability — used on home appliances and children's products. Designers use these signals intentionally to align the product with user expectations; mismatched semantics (e.g. a serious medical device styled like a toy) confuses users and undermines trust.

4. A smartphone and a garden fork are both "tools". Why do users relate to them differently at each of Norman's three levels?

Visceral: the smartphone is designed to look desirable, intimate, premium — users are drawn to it visually. The garden fork is designed to look sturdy and purposeful — visual appeal is functional, not seductive. Behavioural: the smartphone's smooth responsive interaction is designed to feel pleasurable; the fork's satisfaction comes from the tactile feedback of effective work (soil yielding). Reflective: smartphone ownership carries identity signals (status, taste, values); a garden fork rarely does, unless the user values craft or heritage tools. Different product categories engage the three levels very differently.
